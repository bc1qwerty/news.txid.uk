On March 10, 2026, Foundry USA mined block 939,999 and with it, the 20,000,000th bitcoin entered circulation. No press conference. No countdown timer. The milestone passed in the time it took to validate a block — roughly ten minutes — and the network moved on to block 940,000 as if nothing had happened.

But something had happened. Ninety-five percent of all bitcoin that will ever exist is now in the hands of someone — or lost to no one. The remaining five percent, approximately 1,000,000 BTC, will be distributed to miners over the next 114 years, in amounts that halve every four years until the final satoshi is issued sometime around the year 2140.

This is not a rounding error in monetary history. It is the completion of a supply schedule that has been running, uninterrupted, since January 3, 2009 — and it changes the math on scarcity in ways that most people have not yet internalized.

## The Numbers

Bitcoin's supply schedule is one of the few things in economics that is genuinely fixed. Not targeted. Not aspirational. Not subject to committee review. Fixed.

| Milestone | Date | Time to Mine |
|---|---|---|
| First 10 million BTC | Jan 2009 – Nov 2012 | ~4 years |
| Next 5 million BTC | Nov 2012 – Jul 2016 | ~4 years |
| Next 2.5 million BTC | Jul 2016 – May 2020 | ~4 years |
| Next 1.25 million BTC | May 2020 – Apr 2025 | ~5 years |
| 19M → 20M BTC | Apr 2025 – Mar 2026 | ~11 months |
| Final 1 million BTC | Mar 2026 – ~2140 | **~114 years** |

The asymmetry is deliberate. Satoshi Nakamoto designed Bitcoin's emission curve as a geometric series — each halving cuts the rate in half, creating an ever-steeper approach toward the 21 million cap. The first half of all bitcoins was mined in four years. The last million will take more than a century.

At the current rate of 3.125 BTC per block (approximately 450 BTC per day), miners will produce roughly 164,250 BTC per year. After the 2028 halving, that drops to ~82,125. After 2032, ~41,063. Each cycle makes new supply less relevant to the total — and more precious to those competing for it.

## The Supply That Isn't There

Here is the part that makes the 20 million milestone more significant than it appears: a meaningful fraction of those 20 million coins no longer exist in any practical sense.

Chainalysis estimates that between 3.0 and 3.7 million BTC are permanently inaccessible — locked in wallets whose keys have been lost, sent to provably unspendable addresses, or belonging to people who have died without passing on their credentials.

| Category | Estimated BTC |
|---|---|
| Satoshi's untouched coins | ~1,100,000 |
| Verified lost (dead addresses, proven burns) | ~1,200,000 |
| Estimated lost (dormant >10 years, no movement) | 700,000–1,400,000 |
| **Total estimated inaccessible** | **3,000,000–3,700,000** |

If we take the midpoint — 3.35 million BTC effectively destroyed — then the circulating supply is not 20 million. It is closer to **16.65 million**. And the 1 million coins yet to be mined represent not 5% of the eventual total, but closer to **5.7% of the effective supply**.

This is not an academic distinction. Every lost bitcoin increases the scarcity of every remaining bitcoin. Unlike gold, which can be recovered from shipwrecks or recycled from electronics, a lost bitcoin is lost with mathematical finality. There is no recovery operation. The entropy is permanent.

## Satoshi's Coins

The largest known stash of inaccessible bitcoin belongs to Bitcoin's creator. Approximately 1.1 million BTC — mined in the earliest months of the network — sit in addresses attributed to Satoshi Nakamoto. Not a single satoshi has ever moved from these wallets.

At current prices, Satoshi's holdings are worth approximately $75 billion. This would make Bitcoin's anonymous creator one of the wealthiest individuals on Earth — if the coins could be spent. But after 17 years of complete inactivity, the consensus view is that these coins will never move. Whether Satoshi lost the keys, chose not to spend them, or is no longer alive is unknown. The practical effect is the same: 1.1 million BTC are functionally removed from the supply.

If Satoshi's coins ever did move, it would be the most significant event in Bitcoin's history — not because of the selling pressure (which would be enormous), but because it would shatter the assumption of permanent scarcity that underpins the asset's monetary premium.

The probability appears negligible. But it is not zero, and that non-zero probability is itself priced into the market.

## What 95% Mined Means for Miners

For miners, the 20 million milestone is a reminder that their business model has an expiration date — not imminent, but visible on the horizon.

The block subsidy currently accounts for approximately 84% of miner revenue. Transaction fees make up the remaining 16%. Over the next several halvings, this ratio must invert for the network's security model to remain viable.

| Year | Block Reward | Annual Issuance | Fee Revenue Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2026 | 3.125 BTC | ~164,250 BTC | 16% of revenue |
| 2028 | 1.5625 BTC | ~82,125 BTC | Must grow significantly |
| 2032 | 0.78125 BTC | ~41,063 BTC | Must approach 50%+ |
| 2036 | 0.390625 BTC | ~20,531 BTC | Must dominate |

The transition from subsidy-dependent to fee-dependent security is Bitcoin's most important long-term challenge. If transaction fees do not grow to replace the declining block reward, miners will have less economic incentive to secure the network, potentially reducing hashrate and security.

The optimistic view: as Bitcoin's value increases and transaction volume grows (particularly through Layer 2 settlement), fees will naturally fill the gap. Lightning Network channel opens and closes, Liquid Federation transactions, and on-chain settlement of larger values all generate fees.

The pessimistic view: fees have never sustained mining economics alone, and there is no guarantee they will. The block space market is constrained by the 4MB weight limit, and fee pressure during congestion leads to user complaints, not celebration.

Both views are defensible. The answer will unfold over decades, not quarters.

## The Hardest Money Ever Created

As of today, Bitcoin's annual inflation rate is approximately **0.83%**. For context:

| Asset | Annual Supply Increase |
|---|---|
| US Dollar (M2) | ~6–7% |
| Gold | ~1.5–2.0% |
| **Bitcoin (2026)** | **~0.83%** |
| Bitcoin (post-2028) | ~0.41% |
| Bitcoin (post-2032) | ~0.21% |

Bitcoin is already harder than gold by supply issuance. After the 2028 halving, it will be harder by a factor of four. After 2032, by a factor of eight. This trajectory is not a projection or a forecast — it is code that has already been written, reviewed, and running without modification for seventeen years.

No central bank can make this claim. No commodity can make this claim. The hardness of Bitcoin's money is not a policy choice that can be reversed by a committee. It is an emergent property of a network that no single entity controls.

## What Comes Next

The 20 millionth bitcoin was mined without ceremony because Bitcoin does not do ceremony. The protocol does not celebrate milestones. It simply continues producing blocks, adjusting difficulty, and following its emission schedule with the indifference of mathematics.

But for anyone paying attention, the milestone is worth pausing on. The supply curve is now in its steepest phase of deceleration. Each year that passes produces fewer new coins than the year before. Each lost wallet makes the remaining supply scarcer. Each halving tightens the flow further.

One million bitcoins remain to be mined. At current rates, half of those will be produced in the next six years. The other half will take more than a century. The window of abundant new supply is not just closing — it is, by any historical standard, already closed.

The 20 millionth coin was mined on a Monday. The network did not pause. The difficulty adjusted. The mempool cleared. And 450 new bitcoins entered circulation that day, as they will tomorrow, and the day after, each one slightly more scarce than the last.
