There is a particular irony in the fact that the first institutions to receive Hong Kong's stablecoin licenses are the same banks that already print the city's physical currency. HSBC and Standard Chartered — two of the three note-issuing banks authorized by the Hong Kong Monetary Authority to produce the paper banknotes that fill the city's ATMs and cash registers — are now poised to become the first licensed issuers of Hong Kong dollar-backed digital tokens.

The expected license grants, anticipated around March 24, 2026, mark the culmination of a regulatory process that began with the passage of the Stablecoins Ordinance, which took effect in August 2025. The HKMA has received 36 formal applications under the framework — a number that reflects both the commercial appetite for regulated stablecoin issuance and the seriousness with which Hong Kong is pursuing its ambition to become Asia's primary hub for digital asset regulation.

This is not an experiment. It is a deliberate policy decision by a financial center that has calculated, correctly or otherwise, that the future of money includes programmable digital tokens issued under state supervision.

## The Licensing Framework

The Stablecoins Ordinance establishes one of the world's most prescriptive frameworks for stablecoin issuance. Its central requirement — and its most significant differentiator from competing regulatory regimes — is the mandate that reserves backing licensed stablecoins must consist exclusively of High Quality Liquid Assets.

HQLA, as defined by international banking standards under Basel III, includes cash, central bank reserves, and high-grade government securities. It does not include corporate bonds, commercial paper, money market fund shares, or the miscellaneous mix of assets that have historically backed the largest stablecoins in the market.

| Requirement | Hong Kong (Stablecoins Ordinance) | EU (MiCA) | US (Proposed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reserve composition | HQLA only | Cash + low-risk assets | Varies by bill |
| Issuer type restriction | Licensed entities only | E-money institutions, credit institutions | Unclear |
| Audit frequency | Monthly disclosure | Quarterly | Not yet defined |
| Redemption guarantee | At par, on demand | At par, on demand | Varies |
| Sandbox participation | Required for first batch | Not required | No federal sandbox |

Hong Kong is one of the first jurisdictions globally to require HQLA-only reserves — a standard that effectively prohibits the kind of reserve composition that has historically created systemic risk in the stablecoin market. Tether, the largest stablecoin by market capitalization, spent years disclosing reserve compositions that included commercial paper, secured loans, and other assets of varying liquidity and credit quality. The Hong Kong framework eliminates this ambiguity entirely: if it is not HQLA, it cannot back a licensed stablecoin.

The strictness of this requirement explains why the HKMA has prioritized note-issuing banks for its first batch of approvals. HSBC and Standard Chartered already manage massive portfolios of HQLA as part of their banking operations. They already have the regulatory infrastructure, compliance systems, and auditing relationships necessary to demonstrate HQLA reserve management. For these institutions, stablecoin issuance is an extension of their existing treasury operations, not a new capability to build from scratch.

## The Standard Chartered Joint Venture

Standard Chartered's approach to stablecoin issuance illustrates the hybrid models that are emerging at the intersection of traditional banking and digital assets. Rather than issuing a stablecoin independently, the bank formed a joint venture with two partners: Animoca Brands, a Hong Kong-based Web3 company with extensive blockchain gaming and metaverse operations, and HKT, Hong Kong's dominant telecommunications provider.

The joint venture structure is deliberate. Standard Chartered provides the banking license, regulatory relationships, and reserve management capability. Animoca Brands brings distribution through its Web3 ecosystem and a user base already familiar with digital tokens. HKT offers the payment infrastructure and retail distribution network of a telecoms company with millions of existing customer relationships.

| JV Partner | Role | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Chartered | Banking & compliance | Regulatory license, HQLA reserve management, institutional credibility |
| Animoca Brands | Web3 distribution | Blockchain ecosystem access, digital-native user base |
| HKT (telecoms) | Retail distribution | Payment infrastructure, 6M+ mobile subscribers |

The resulting HKD-backed stablecoin would have something that most existing stablecoins lack: a regulated banking partner managing reserves, a crypto-native distribution channel, and a traditional retail payment network — all under a single licensed entity.

Standard Chartered is not entering this market cold. The bank participated in the HKMA's stablecoin issuer sandbox in 2024, a program that allowed selected institutions to test stablecoin issuance under regulatory supervision before the Stablecoins Ordinance took effect. The sandbox experience gave Standard Chartered — and the HKMA — an opportunity to identify operational issues, stress-test reserve management procedures, and develop the regulatory reporting frameworks that would eventually become part of the formal licensing requirements.

## The Competitive Landscape

Hong Kong's stablecoin licensing program does not exist in a vacuum. It is one move in a multi-jurisdictional competition to establish the regulatory framework that will govern the next generation of digital money. The competitors are known, and each brings different strengths and constraints.

**Singapore** has taken a principles-based approach through the Monetary Authority of Singapore's Payment Services Act. Singapore's framework is more flexible than Hong Kong's — it does not mandate HQLA-only reserves — but it benefits from Singapore's established position as a fintech hub and its deep pool of technology talent. Several major stablecoin issuers, including Circle and Paxos, maintain significant operations in Singapore.

**Dubai** has pursued an aggressive "regulatory sandbox" strategy through the Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority and the Dubai International Financial Centre. Dubai's approach emphasizes speed and flexibility, attracting companies that want to launch products quickly without the compliance burden of more established financial centers. The trade-off is regulatory credibility — a Dubai license does not carry the same institutional weight as one from Hong Kong or Singapore.

**The European Union** implemented MiCA — the Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation — which took full effect in 2025. MiCA provides the most comprehensive stablecoin framework by geographic scope, covering 27 member states with a single set of rules. However, MiCA's reserve requirements are less stringent than Hong Kong's HQLA mandate, and the regulation has been criticized for creating compliance costs that disadvantage European issuers relative to competitors in less regulated jurisdictions.

| Jurisdiction | Framework | Reserve Standard | Key Advantage | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hong Kong | Stablecoins Ordinance | HQLA only | Strictest reserves, institutional credibility | Smaller market, China relationship uncertainty |
| Singapore | Payment Services Act | Principles-based | Flexibility, fintech ecosystem | Less prescriptive reserves |
| Dubai | VARA framework | Sandbox-based | Speed, regulatory flexibility | Credibility concerns |
| EU | MiCA | Cash + low-risk assets | Largest geographic scope | Compliance costs, bureaucratic |
| United States | No federal framework | N/A | Largest market | Regulatory fragmentation |

The United States remains conspicuously absent from this table as a regulated stablecoin jurisdiction. Multiple stablecoin bills have been introduced in Congress, but as of March 2026, no comprehensive federal framework has been enacted. This regulatory vacuum has been a gift to competing jurisdictions — every month that the US delays is a month in which Hong Kong, Singapore, and the EU can establish facts on the ground.

## Why Note-Issuing Banks Matter

The HKMA's decision to prioritize HSBC and Standard Chartered is not merely administrative convenience. It reflects a specific philosophy about the relationship between stablecoins and the existing monetary system.

Hong Kong's currency regime is unique. The Hong Kong dollar is pegged to the US dollar through a currency board system, and physical banknotes are issued not by the central bank but by three commercial banks: HSBC, Standard Chartered, and Bank of China (Hong Kong). These banks hold US dollar reserves with the HKMA and issue HK dollar banknotes against those reserves. The system has operated since the colonial era and has survived multiple financial crises, including the 1997 Asian financial crisis, the 2008 global financial crisis, and the COVID-19 pandemic.

A HKD-backed stablecoin issued by HSBC or Standard Chartered is, in structural terms, a digital extension of this existing system. The bank holds HQLA reserves — predominantly US dollar-denominated assets — and issues digital tokens denominated in Hong Kong dollars against those reserves. The mechanics are functionally identical to physical banknote issuance, updated for digital infrastructure.

This parallel is not accidental. The HKMA has deliberately designed the stablecoin licensing framework to mirror the existing note issuance system, creating regulatory consistency between physical and digital forms of the Hong Kong dollar. The result is a stablecoin that inherits the institutional credibility and regulatory oversight of a system that has operated reliably for decades.

## The 36 Applications

Beyond the first batch of bank-led issuers, the 36 applications filed with the HKMA represent a diverse cross-section of the digital asset industry. While specific applicants have not been publicly disclosed, the range is understood to include traditional financial institutions, crypto-native companies, and technology firms.

The volume of applications suggests that the market views Hong Kong's licensing framework as commercially viable despite its stringent requirements. A regulatory regime that was too restrictive would have attracted few applications. A regime that was too permissive would have attracted applications from companies that could not realistically meet the standards. Thirty-six applications suggest a framework that is demanding but achievable — which is precisely the balance a regulator should aim for.

Not all 36 applications will result in licenses. The HKMA has indicated that it will grant licenses in batches, with the note-issuing banks receiving priority in the first round. Subsequent rounds will evaluate applications based on the applicant's financial resources, technological capability, compliance infrastructure, and the viability of its business model. The HKMA has not disclosed a timeline for processing all 36 applications, but market participants expect the process to extend through the remainder of 2026.

## Implications for the Global Stablecoin Market

The global stablecoin market has grown to exceed $300 billion in total market capitalization, dominated by Tether (USDT) and Circle's USDC. Both are US dollar-denominated, and both are issued by companies that, despite significant improvements in transparency, operate without comprehensive regulatory frameworks equivalent to what Hong Kong is now implementing.

Hong Kong's licensed stablecoins will initially be a rounding error in this market. A HKD-backed stablecoin, regardless of issuer, will face the inherent limitation of being denominated in a currency that is not the global reserve currency. The demand for HKD stablecoins will be driven primarily by Hong Kong's domestic market, regional trade settlement, and use cases specific to the HKD currency zone.

But the significance is not in the size of the initial market. It is in the precedent. If Hong Kong demonstrates that HQLA-backed, bank-issued, fully regulated stablecoins can operate commercially — generating revenue for issuers while providing safety for holders — the model will be replicated. Other jurisdictions will face pressure to adopt similar standards, and issuers operating under less stringent regimes will face questions about why their reserves do not meet the HQLA standard.

| Stablecoin Market Segment | Current Leaders | HK Impact |
|---|---|---|
| USD-denominated | Tether (USDT), Circle (USDC) | Indirect — raises reserve standard expectations |
| EUR-denominated | Circle (EURC), Societe Generale (EURCV) | Indirect — MiCA already regulates |
| HKD-denominated | None (new market) | Direct — first regulated issuance |
| Multi-currency | Limited | Potential future expansion |

The competitive pressure cuts in both directions. Hong Kong's strict framework may attract institutional users who prioritize safety, while driving more speculative or cost-sensitive users toward stablecoins issued under lighter regulatory regimes. The market may ultimately segment: HQLA-backed stablecoins for institutional settlement and regulated commerce, and less strictly backed tokens for trading, DeFi, and emerging market use cases where regulatory compliance is less important than accessibility.

## The China Question

Any discussion of Hong Kong's financial policy must address the relationship with mainland China. Hong Kong operates under the "one country, two systems" framework, which grants the city autonomous monetary and regulatory authority. The HKMA is not the People's Bank of China. Hong Kong's financial regulations are not subject to mainland approval.

But political reality is more complex than legal frameworks suggest. China has banned cryptocurrency trading and mining on the mainland. It is simultaneously developing the digital yuan (e-CNY) as a state-controlled digital currency. Hong Kong's embrace of regulated stablecoins appears to contradict Beijing's stance — unless it is understood as complementary.

The more plausible interpretation is that Hong Kong serves as China's controlled experiment in digital asset regulation. If regulated stablecoins prove commercially viable and systemically safe in Hong Kong, the lessons can inform mainland policy. If they prove problematic, the damage is contained within Hong Kong's financial system without contaminating the mainland. This "one country, two systems" approach to financial innovation mirrors the broader political framework: experimentation at the periphery, control at the center.

## What Comes Next

The immediate next steps are procedural. The HKMA will formally grant licenses to the first batch of approved applicants. HSBC and the Standard Chartered-led joint venture will begin the operational process of launching their stablecoins — establishing reserve accounts, deploying smart contracts, integrating with payment networks, and onboarding initial users.

The more consequential developments will unfold over the following twelve to eighteen months. The key questions are straightforward but their answers are not yet knowable. Will institutional users in Asia adopt HKD stablecoins for trade settlement, creating genuine demand beyond retail speculation? Will the HQLA reserve standard prove commercially viable, or will issuers find that the cost of maintaining exclusively high-quality reserves erodes margins to the point of unviability? Will competing jurisdictions adopt similar standards, creating a global floor for stablecoin reserve quality?

And the question that hangs over all of it: will the 36 applicants who filed under the Stablecoins Ordinance still want their licenses twelve months from now, or will the market have moved in a direction that makes Hong Kong's careful, conservative framework look like a regulatory artifact of a moment that has already passed?

The answer depends on whether the market values safety over speed — and on that question, the stablecoin industry's history provides ample evidence for both sides.
