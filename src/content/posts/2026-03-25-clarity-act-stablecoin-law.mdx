The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act — known as the CLARITY Act — has been stalled for months over a single issue: whether crypto exchanges should be permitted to pay interest on stablecoin holdings. That impasse may be over. Senator Thom Tillis (R-NC) and Senator Angela Alsobrooks (D-MD) have reached a tentative bipartisan deal on the stablecoin yield provision, removing the primary obstacle to what would be the first comprehensive market structure framework for digital assets in the United States.

The deal, described by people familiar with the negotiations as "99% of the way to resolution," bars rewards on passive stablecoin balances — a concession to banking interests — while preserving the broader market structure framework that the crypto industry has spent years pursuing. Senate Banking Committee markup is targeted for the second half of April, after the Easter recess ends on April 13. If that timeline holds, the CLARITY Act could reach a full Senate vote before summer.

This is the legislation that defines what a digital asset is, which agency regulates it, and how companies can legally operate in the United States. Everything else in crypto regulation — enforcement actions, licensing frameworks, investor protections — flows downstream from these foundational questions. The CLARITY Act is, by any measure, the most consequential piece of crypto legislation since the GENIUS Act was signed into law in July 2025.

## The Legislative Landscape: Where Things Stand

Understanding the CLARITY Act requires understanding the regulatory architecture that already exists and the gap it is designed to fill.

The GENIUS Act (Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for US Stablecoins) was signed into law on July 18, 2025, after passing the Senate 68-30 and the House 308-122. Those margins — roughly two-thirds in both chambers — reflect genuine bipartisan consensus on the narrow question of stablecoin regulation. The GENIUS Act established a federal licensing framework for stablecoin issuers, reserve requirements, and redemption rights for holders.

| Legislation | Status | Scope | Key Dates |
|---|---|---|---|
| GENIUS Act | Signed into law | Stablecoin issuance, reserves, redemption | Passed: July 18, 2025; Regulations due: July 18, 2026 |
| CLARITY Act | In committee | Comprehensive market structure: asset classification, SEC/CFTC jurisdiction, exchange registration | Markup target: late April 2026 |

The GENIUS Act regulations are due by July 18, 2026 — one year after enactment. Federal agencies are currently writing the implementation rules, and the industry is operating in a transition period where the law exists but its operational details remain undefined. This regulatory uncertainty is one reason the CLARITY Act matters: it would provide the market structure framework that gives the GENIUS Act's stablecoin provisions operational context within the broader digital asset ecosystem.

The gap the CLARITY Act addresses is fundamental. As of March 2026, the United States still lacks a statutory answer to the most basic question in digital asset regulation: is a given token a security, a commodity, or something else entirely? The SEC has argued through enforcement actions that most tokens are securities. The CFTC has claimed jurisdiction over tokens that function as commodities. Courts have produced inconsistent rulings. Market participants operate in a compliance gray zone where the rules depend on which agency decides to act and which legal theory a particular judge finds persuasive.

The CLARITY Act proposes to resolve this by establishing a statutory classification framework — a set of criteria that determines whether a digital asset falls under SEC or CFTC jurisdiction based on its characteristics and use, not based on ad hoc enforcement decisions.

## The Stablecoin Yield Problem

The provision that nearly killed the CLARITY Act is narrower than the bill's overall scope, but it touches one of the most politically charged intersections in financial regulation: the boundary between banking and non-banking financial services.

The question is straightforward: should crypto exchanges and stablecoin platforms be permitted to pay interest or yield on stablecoin deposits?

From the crypto industry's perspective, the answer is obviously yes. Stablecoins are digital representations of dollars. If a bank can pay interest on dollar deposits, a crypto platform should be able to pay yield on stablecoin holdings. The technology is different; the economic function is the same.

From the banking industry's perspective, the answer is obviously no. Paying interest on deposits is a core banking function that comes with a specific set of regulatory obligations: FDIC insurance, capital requirements, reserve ratios, examination by federal regulators. Allowing crypto platforms to offer yield-bearing stablecoin accounts without these obligations creates an unlevel playing field that could drain deposits from regulated banks into less-regulated crypto platforms.

| Stakeholder | Position on Stablecoin Yield | Core Argument |
|---|---|---|
| Crypto exchanges | Allow yield payments | Economic equivalence with bank deposits |
| Banking lobby | Prohibit yield payments | Regulatory arbitrage; unfair competition |
| Federal regulators | Cautious | Consumer protection; systemic risk concerns |
| Senate Republicans (majority) | Allow with restrictions | Innovation; competitive positioning |
| Senate Democrats (majority) | Prohibit or heavily restrict | Consumer protection; bank stability |

The political alignment cut across the usual crypto-friendly vs. crypto-skeptical divide. Several pro-crypto senators who supported the broader CLARITY Act were sympathetic to banking concerns on the yield question, while some moderate Democrats who had reservations about the bill's overall scope were willing to support it if the yield provision was removed or restricted.

The result was a stalemate that persisted for months. The CLARITY Act had broad support in principle but could not advance because the stablecoin yield question could not be resolved without alienating one coalition or the other.

## The Tillis-Alsobrooks Accord

The breakthrough came from an unlikely pairing. Senator Tillis, a Republican from North Carolina with a pro-business record and a state that hosts a significant financial services industry, and Senator Alsobrooks, a first-term Democrat from Maryland, negotiated a compromise that threads the needle.

The core of the accord: passive stablecoin balances held at exchanges or custodians may not earn rewards or yield. This bars the most direct form of interest-bearing stablecoin accounts — the arrangement that banking lobbyists most strongly opposed. A user who holds USDC in a Coinbase account, for example, would not earn interest on that balance simply for holding it.

The accord reportedly preserves the ability for stablecoin issuers to offer yield through mechanisms that require active participation — staking in DeFi protocols, lending, or other activities that involve the holder taking on risk. The distinction between "passive" and "active" yield is the legal innovation that makes the deal work. It gives banks comfort that crypto platforms will not replicate the core deposit-taking function while giving the crypto industry a path to yield-bearing products that require user action beyond simply holding a balance.

The compromise is imperfect. The definition of "passive" will inevitably be litigated and tested. A platform that requires users to click a single button to "opt in" to a yield program could argue the balance is no longer passive. The regulatory implementation will require precise line-drawing that the accord itself does not fully specify.

But political compromises are not designed to be perfect. They are designed to be sufficient — to give enough to each side that the legislation can advance. The Tillis-Alsobrooks accord appears to have met that threshold. Crypto and banking representatives were headed to Capitol Hill on March 23 to review the deal details, and early signals suggest both sides find the framework workable.

## The Remaining Obstacles

The stablecoin yield resolution removes the largest obstacle but not the only one. Several significant issues remain unresolved as the CLARITY Act moves toward markup:

| Outstanding Issue | Status | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|
| Stablecoin yield | Tentatively resolved (Tillis-Alsobrooks) | Low (deal in place) |
| DeFi treatment | Unresolved | High |
| Ethics provisions | Unresolved | Medium |
| SEC/CFTC jurisdictional boundaries | Partially resolved | Medium |
| Remaining political friction | Present | Variable |

**DeFi treatment** is the most technically challenging question. Decentralized finance protocols operate without a central entity — there is no company to register, no officer to hold accountable, no jurisdiction in which the protocol is domiciled. The CLARITY Act must define how, or whether, regulatory requirements apply to protocols that are genuinely decentralized. The crypto industry argues that truly decentralized protocols are more like open-source software than financial services companies and should be regulated accordingly. Regulators argue that the label "decentralized" is often applied to protocols that have identifiable teams, governance token holders with concentrated voting power, and centralized front-end interfaces.

The current draft reportedly includes a "sufficient decentralization" test — a set of criteria that, if met, exempts a protocol from certain registration requirements. The details of this test are among the most contested provisions in the bill, and they may not be fully resolved before the markup.

**Ethics provisions** relate to the broader political environment in which the CLARITY Act is being negotiated. Several senators have raised concerns about potential conflicts of interest, particularly regarding legislators and their families who hold digital asset investments. These provisions are not specific to market structure per se, but they have become a condition for some Democratic senators' support.

**Jurisdictional questions** between the SEC and CFTC have been partially addressed in the current draft but remain a source of inter-agency tension. The CLARITY Act's classification framework would effectively transfer regulatory authority over certain digital assets from the SEC to the CFTC — a reallocation of power that the SEC has resisted. The political dynamics of this bureaucratic turf war should not be underestimated; agencies fight hard to preserve jurisdiction, and the SEC has a larger budget, more staff, and more political influence than the CFTC.

## The Timeline

Senator Cynthia Lummis, one of the Senate's most vocal crypto advocates, has confirmed the markup timeline: the Senate Banking Committee is targeting the second half of April, after Congress returns from Easter recess on April 13. Chairman Tim Scott is pushing for final passage of market structure legislation, and the committee staff has been working through the recess to prepare the markup draft.

| Milestone | Target Date | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Tillis-Alsobrooks yield accord | Mid-March 2026 | Tentatively agreed |
| Industry/bank review of deal details | March 23, 2026 | Completed |
| Easter recess ends | April 13, 2026 | Fixed |
| Senate Banking Committee markup | Late April 2026 | Targeted |
| Committee vote | Late April/early May | Dependent on markup |
| Full Senate vote | TBD (summer 2026 target) | Aspirational |
| House consideration | TBD | Separate timeline |
| Presidential signature | TBD | Unknown |

The timeline is ambitious. Even if the markup proceeds on schedule in late April, the path from committee to full Senate vote to House consideration to presidential signature involves months of procedural steps, floor negotiations, and potential amendments. The GENIUS Act took approximately eight months from committee markup to presidential signature. If the CLARITY Act follows a similar pace, final enactment would occur in late 2026 or early 2027.

This assumes no major disruptions. The Hormuz crisis, the presidential campaign cycle, and the general dysfunction of Congress all represent potential delays. Legislation that is not signed into law before the end of the current Congress (January 2027) dies and must be reintroduced from scratch.

## Why This Matters: The Market Structure Gap

The importance of the CLARITY Act extends beyond regulatory clarity for its own sake. The absence of a market structure framework has concrete economic consequences that the industry quantifies in lost business, legal costs, and offshore migration.

Without a statutory classification system, every digital asset project faces a fundamental question: are we a security or not? The answer determines which regulator oversees the project, which registration requirements apply, which investors can participate, and which exchanges can list the token. Under the current enforcement-driven regime, the answer often depends on whether the SEC decides to bring an enforcement action — a determination that is made after the fact, case by case, with limited predictability.

This uncertainty imposes costs. Legal fees for crypto startups routinely exceed those of comparable traditional finance startups because the regulatory framework must be navigated without a map. Institutional investors — pension funds, endowments, insurance companies — remain largely on the sidelines because their compliance departments cannot definitively determine whether a given asset complies with applicable regulations. Exchanges face the choice of listing tokens that may later be declared unregistered securities or refusing to list tokens that may be perfectly legal.

The CLARITY Act would not solve every problem, but it would replace the current system — where classification depends on enforcement discretion — with a statutory framework where classification depends on objective criteria. That is a fundamental improvement, regardless of where the specific lines are drawn.

## The Broader Regulatory Architecture

If the CLARITY Act passes, the United States will have a two-pillar regulatory framework for digital assets:

**Pillar 1: The GENIUS Act (stablecoin regulation).** Already law. Establishes federal licensing for stablecoin issuers, reserve requirements, redemption rights, and the regulatory relationship between state and federal oversight. Regulations due by July 18, 2026.

**Pillar 2: The CLARITY Act (market structure).** In progress. Would establish asset classification criteria, SEC/CFTC jurisdictional boundaries, exchange and broker-dealer registration requirements, and investor protection standards for the broader digital asset market.

Together, these two laws would provide the most comprehensive digital asset regulatory framework in any major economy — more detailed than the EU's MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets) regulation and more permissive than many Asian frameworks. The competitive implications for the US digital asset industry would be significant: clear rules attract capital, talent, and institutional participation in ways that regulatory ambiguity cannot.

| Regulatory Framework | Jurisdiction | Scope | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| GENIUS Act | United States | Stablecoins | Enacted (July 2025) |
| CLARITY Act | United States | Market structure | In committee |
| MiCA | European Union | Comprehensive | Enforcement began 2025 |
| FSA framework | Japan | Comprehensive | Operational |
| PSA framework | Singapore | Comprehensive | Operational |

The US has lagged its major economic peers in establishing digital asset regulation. The CLARITY Act would close much of that gap. Whether the political will exists to move it through both chambers and to a presidential signature before this Congress expires remains the central question.

## What Success Looks Like

If the CLARITY Act reaches the president's desk and is signed into law, several things change immediately:

First, the SEC's enforcement-driven approach to classification becomes constrained by statute. The agency can still bring enforcement actions, but it must do so within the framework established by the law rather than under the open-ended Howey test applied to novel technology. This is the change the industry cares about most.

Second, exchanges and custodians gain a registration pathway that does not require them to operate as traditional broker-dealers or national securities exchanges. The current regulatory structure forces crypto platforms into categories designed for equities and bonds — a square-peg-round-hole problem that the CLARITY Act addresses with crypto-specific registration categories.

Third, the jurisdictional line between the SEC and CFTC becomes statutory rather than negotiated. This eliminates the inter-agency ambiguity that has allowed both regulators to claim or disclaim authority over the same assets depending on the political moment.

Fourth, the "sufficient decentralization" framework — if it survives the markup process — creates a legal basis for DeFi protocols to operate in the US without registering as traditional financial institutions. This is the most legally novel provision in the bill and the one most likely to be modified during the legislative process.

## The Political Reality

The Tillis-Alsobrooks accord represents a significant step, but it is a step, not the finish line. The CLARITY Act must survive committee markup, where any senator on the Banking Committee can offer amendments. It must pass the full Senate, where procedural rules give individual senators significant power to delay or obstruct. It must be reconciled with whatever version the House produces — and the House has its own committee process, its own political dynamics, and its own timeline.

The deal is also fragile. Described as "99% of the way to resolution," it leaves 1% unresolved — and in legislative negotiations, the final 1% often contains the issues that are most politically sensitive and most difficult to compromise on. The remaining friction is described as "political" rather than substantive, which in Washington means it relates to credit, timing, and electoral positioning rather than policy substance. These are not trivial considerations; they have killed legislation before.

Chairman Scott's commitment to pushing the bill forward is a positive signal. The GENIUS Act demonstrated that bipartisan crypto legislation can pass with large margins when the political conditions align. The question is whether the conditions that produced a 68-30 Senate vote on stablecoins in 2025 can be replicated for the broader and more contentious market structure bill in 2026.

The industry is cautiously optimistic. The stablecoin yield issue was the largest obstacle, and it appears to have been cleared. The remaining issues are significant but negotiable. The timeline is tight but achievable. For the first time since the CLARITY Act was introduced, passage before the end of this Congress appears more likely than not — though "more likely than not" is a long way from "certain."

The next six weeks will determine whether this optimism is justified. The markup in late April will reveal whether the Tillis-Alsobrooks accord holds, whether the DeFi and ethics provisions can be resolved, and whether the committee can produce a bill that commands the bipartisan support necessary for floor passage. The crypto industry has been waiting years for a market structure law. The wait may soon be over — or it may extend once again.
